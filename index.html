Persian Pronunciation Guide for English Speakers

Persian has five basic vowel signs (niqqud) and a relatively small set of consonants, some of which do not exist in English. This guide uses IPA and notes important pronunciation distinctions between Modern Israeli Persian (the standard today) and traditional/liturgical Persian. Examples are shown with Persian script, niqqud (vowel points), and transliteration.

Persian Letter Pronunciation Symbol Example (Persian) Similar English Sound
ַ (pataḥ) /a/ בַּיִת [ˈbajit] "house" Like "a" in "father"
ֵ (tsere) /e/ סֵפֶר [ˈsefer] "book" Between "bait" and "bet" (without glide)
ִ (ḥiriq) /i/ מִי [mi] "who" Like "ee" in "machine"
ֹ (ḥolam) /o/ יוֹם [jom] "day" Like "o" in "story"
וּ (shuruq) /u/ שׁוּק [ʃuk] "market" Like "oo" in "food"
ח (ḥet) /χ/ or /ħ/ (traditional) חַג [χag] "holiday" Scottish "loch" (no English equivalent)
כ/ך (khaf) /χ/ כָּל [kol] "all" As above
ק (qof) /k/ קָטָן [kaˈtan] "small" Like "k" in "kite" but deeper in the throat
ע (ʿayin) /ʔ/ or silent in Modern Persian עֵץ [ets] "tree" Glottal stop (like the break in "uh-oh")

Stress in Persian

In Modern Persian, stress usually falls on the last syllable of a word, but many common words preserve older patterns with penultimate stress. Unlike Spanish, Persian does not use written accents to mark stress. Examples: בַּיִת [ˈbajit] (penultimate), לָכוּד [laˈχud] (final).

Dialect Notes at a Glance

Modern Israeli Persian: Standard in schools/media; guttural ע and ח often weakened.
Sephardic tradition: Historical basis of modern pronunciation; clear distinction of vowels.
Ashkenazi Persian: Still used in prayer; stress often moves to penultimate syllable; some vowel shifts (e.g. kamatz → /o/).
Mizrahi traditions: Preserve pharyngeal sounds [ħ, ʕ] for ח and ע.

Helpful Symbols and Conventions

IPA brackets [ ] indicate detailed sounds. Slashes / / indicate phonemes. Persian spelling does not always reflect vowel quality, since most vowels are not written without niqqud. Letters ב, ג, ד, כ, פ, ת may have a "hard" [b g d k p t] or "soft" [v ɣ ð χ f θ] form historically, but only ב/כ/פ remain distinct today (as [v], [χ], [f]).
